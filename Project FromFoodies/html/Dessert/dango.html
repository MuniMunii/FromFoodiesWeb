Ingredients

Dango

  • 1 cup mochiko (sweet rice flour)
  • 1/2 cup hot water
  • Food coloring (optional)
  • Mizuame or sweet soy sauce
  • Kinako (roasted soybean flour), for serving (optional)

Story Behind Dango

Dango is a traditional Japanese sweet made from rice flour and often served on a skewer. The story behind dango is rooted in Japanese history and culture. According to legend, the origins of dango can be traced back to the Edo period in Japan (1603-1868), when they were first introduced as a snack for samurai warriors. At the time, dango was made with millet flour and served as a portable, high-energy food that could be easily carried and consumed on the battlefield. Over time, dango became more widely consumed and developed into a popular snack throughout Japan. Today, there are many variations of dango, including different flavors and toppings. In addition to being a beloved sweet treat, dango is also a symbol of Japanese culture and tradition. The three balls of dango commonly served on a skewer are said to represent the three elements of life: earth, water, and fire. Overall, the story behind dango reflects the importance of food in Japanese history and culture, as well as the enduring popularity of this delicious sweet snack.

How To Make

Dango

  1. In a mixing bowl, combine the mochiko and hot water. Mix well until the dough comes together.
  2. If using food coloring, divide the dough into portions and knead in the desired amount of food coloring to achieve the desired color.
  3. Divide the dough into small portions and roll each portion into a ball.
  4. Boil a pot of water and add the dango balls. Cook until the dango floats to the surface, then cook for an additional 2-3 minutes.
  5. Remove the dango from the pot and transfer to a bowl of cold water for a few seconds.
  6. Drain the dango and skewer them onto bamboo skewers (3-4 balls per skewer).
